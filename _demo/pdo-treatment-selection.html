Picking the right DDR drug for the right patient.

A patient-derived organoid (PDO) is established from a fresh tumour biopsy and split into seven arms — five candidate DDR drugs at clinically achievable concentrations, plus a no-treatment baseline and a vehicle control. After 48 h, POLEX™ measures genome-wide double-strand break burden in each arm. The drug producing the strongest on-target DNA damage at the lowest predicted toxicity becomes the recommended first-line therapy for that specific patient.

DSB burden per treatment arm

Each bar = mean DSB burden across 3 organoid replicates ± SD. Higher = more on-target DNA damage induced by that drug in this patient’s tumour.

How to read this

The control arms (Untreated, DMSO) establish the baseline DSB burden for this organoid. Anything above this line is drug-induced damage.

The recommended drug (highlighted bar) is the one producing the largest DSB increase over baseline. For a DDR-targeted therapy, a strong DSB induction means the patient’s tumour has the molecular vulnerability the drug exploits — a positive functional biomarker independent of genotype.

Weak responses across all arms suggest no monotherapy will be effective; the case should be considered for combination therapy or a clinical trial.

About this view. Conventional cytotoxicity assays (CellTiter-Glo, MTS) report whether organoid cells died, but not why — they conflate on-target DNA damage with generic toxicity. POLEX™ measures DSB burden directly at base resolution across the whole genome (including centromeres and satellite arrays where short-read methods are blind), separating drug efficacy from off-target toxicity and giving the oncologist a mechanism-anchored treatment recommendation.
